# How To Judge Magic Cards

So I came up with this rating system to help my friend out when making new decks, and I figured I might as well share it to see if it helps any other new players. It might be kind of convoluted and maybe unhelpful in a vacuum like this, but it's a general concept which I personally use to pick cards when brewing so maybe it will work for some other weirdos like me out there who need structure and systems to think properly. Also, I plan to make a video series or something similar where I use this rating system to rate every single magic card ever printed (ambitious I know) and post it online to further expound upon this idea and hopefully help people get even better at building decks who dont know how to judge cards very well for themselves yet. If you found this helpful I would appreciate feedback. If not, then that's OK, it's really just for me and my friend anyway, but I'm happy you took the time to read it. Enjoy!

## Impact-

Is the card worth the slot in your deck? Does it provide a significant advantage to help you get ahead or catch up on the board either immediately or over time? Does it provide card advantage? How much does this one card affect the entire game as a whole?

Cards will be compared to other cards that have similar effects but do more or less to alter the game in a measurable way, largely regardless of efficiency or practicality. Mana cost will only be marginally considered when determining factors such as which turn a spell could realistically be the only action taken, and how powerful that action would likely be by that point in the game. Cards will be judged in optimal cases, assuming that a deck is built around them properly with the appropriate support.

Generally, highly impactful spells will require a larger investment, but will also provide a greater return, and will receive a higher grade for contributing to card or board advantage. A high-impact card provides a means to come back from a disadvantageous position or pull far ahead of your opponents in one way or another. Low impact cards are generally low in investment and have little to no impact on the game and provide no practical means to gain an advantageous position on their own.

The worst cards in this category are a liability to play or even include in a deck, and do not provide enough benefits to out-value the card slot they take up. The best cards in this category will provide an accessible and immediate path to victory, usually requiring only to resolve once or survive a turn cycle to provide such advantage as to effectively win the game for you.

1- A card has more negative impact than positive impact for the player. Causes card disadvantage or weakens your board state or survival chances for largely insignificant effect. Worse than wasting a card slot or turn, the card is usually a detriment in your hand or on the field. Ex: Wood Elemental; Alabaster Leech; Sorrow's Path

2- A card has no significant impact on the board state. Usually a waste of a turn and card slot to provide almost no alteration to the game in your favor. These are typically cheap, sub-optimal versions of already weak cards and effects, and are generally unplayable in any format. Ex: Willow Elf; Hornet Sting; Bone Saw

3- A card has a small impact that will usually have little outcome on the game by itself. Causes minor alterations to the board state or survival chances that may be slightly beneficial in typical scenarios, and usually only in conjunction with other actions. These cards are barely useable, even in limited formats. Ex: Spark; Grim Physician; Unholy Strength

4- A card alters the game state in a minor but not insignificant way, usually providing a step towards a more impactful advantage or creating a mild setback for opponents on it's own. The average level of common cards playable in limited formats. Ex: Dawn Gryff; Last Gasp; Cartouche of Knowledge

5- A card has an average impact on the game state, usually providing a reasonable advantage on it's own at any particular point in the game. Typically the lowest level of impact that may be included in most decks of constructed formats. Ex: Thundering Rebuke; Serra Angel; Trial of Solidarity

6- A card with an above-average impact on the board or card state or that provides a relevant advantage or threat to the table. These are usually minor supporting cards for some constructed formats and can be minor win conditions in limited. Ex: Return to Dust; Broodmate Dragon; Dictate of Heliod

7- A card with a somewhat high level of power and impact. These cards will provide a significant and immediate advantage that will require timely action from opponents to mitigate. These cards may be staple support cards in constructed formats and primary win conditions in limited. Ex: Garruk's Horde; Liliana's Mastery; Serpentine Spike

8- A card has a high level of impact on the game. While not overwhelming, this card provides one or more powerful advantages that will be difficult to mitigate. Opponents must usually answer the threat within a turn cycle. These cards may be staple buildaround cards that make up the bulk of constructed formats. Ex: Wrath of God; Stormtide Leviathan; Akroma's Memorial

9- A card has a very powerful impact on the game that will immediately provide a route to victory to its player, and give opponents less than a turn cycle to react before you will win the game. This power level of cards are usually the win conditions for competitive decks in constructed formats. Ex: Ugin, the Spirit Dragon; Craterhoof Behemoth; Planar Cleansing

10- A card is on an unprecedented power level matched by very few other cards in magic's history. These are the most impactful and game-changing cards you could play, and will almost always result in ending the game immediately after resolving. These cards are usually quite expensive to cast and are sometimes banned in one or more formats due to their extreme potential for unbalanced exploitation. Ex: Worldfire; Time Stretch; Emrakul, the Aeons Torn

## Efficiency-

What does this card cost to play? What is its converted mana cost? What does it offer for its CMC? How difficult is it to cast? Does it pass the vanilla test? What is the ratio of stats to costs? Where does this fit on the mana curve and does it belong there?

Cards will be measured and compared to other cards to determine their efficiency. Mana efficiency is the most noticeable victim of "power creep", the phenomenon which dictates that magic cards and decks become better, faster, and more consistent in new sets compared to old sets as time goes on. This means that typically modern cards are more efficient than old ones, though this is not always true.

This category is an easy way to determine which cards that have similar or identical effects are objectively worse or better than each other. When comparing cards, some universal measurement is taken into account which revolves around standardized costs for different types of effects based on cards from magic's history.

For example, the vanilla test is used to determine how efficient in terms of mana a creature is. The baseline for a playable creature with no abilities is that its CMC is less than or equal to half its total power and toughness. This simple analysis, while not perfect, is a good baseline determinant to help rule out especially unplayable jank from a card pool. This test can also be extended and altered slightly to determine the viability of creatures with abilities and other types of spells which have various effects, though adding secondary effects and alternate casting costs to the equation can make things very complicated, indeed.

While this test is helpful, a much more crucial determining factor when analyzing efficiency is the meta of magic as a whole and/or a particular format. The vanilla test, for creatures, has quickly become outdated as more and better magic cards are printed, and many of the best cards playable in most constructed formats today provide extreme efficiency and impact for their cost compared to older cards. As such, the grading scale for efficiency, more so than the other categories, is subject to change and alter as time goes on.

Taking this into account, it is important to know the "meta". Looking at cards that are currently played in a particular format and comparing the costs of casting that card and another similar card, it may be observable that one is objectively better than the other. While every card is different, and they all provide different effects, an experienced player will be able to understand which parts of the card and its effects are the most powerful and relevant, and will be able to use that to determine if its mana cost is worth it.

As mana cost rises, efficiency also becomes more and more important; while low CMC cards can be very good, they are usually not very impactful. Highly efficient cards will be as impactful as higher CMC cards but cost less, and will be just as easy to cast if placed in the right deck. And note that when determining the efficiency of creatures, especially in the vanilla test, power is usually given more weight and importance than toughness, meaning that more aggressively costed creatures are usually considered more efficient.

1- This card has an abysmal casting cost or some other huge requirement and the effect that it has on the game can be attained much more easily by almost any other card with this effect. These cards are objectively worse and more costly to play than anything in any format today. Ex: Natural Spring; Lightning Diadem; Jedit Ojanen

2- This card is very expensive for the effect it offers. Its stats and abilities are simply not worth the cost required to play under most circumstances. There is at least one more efficient card with a similar ability available in most formats and these cards would only be played as a last resort in limited. Ex: Armored Cancrix; Reach of Shadows; The Hive

3- The card is somewhat inefficient and expensive for what it does. These cards are high costed and low powered even in limited, though they are still playable. There are most likely better alternatives, but these cards are viable in slow or casual formats when options are few. Ex: Godhunter Octopus; Jayemdae Tome; Open Fire

4- A card is slightly less than average in terms of efficiency. This is the most commonly played power level in limited formats. There are more efficient versions of these cards, though usually at higher rarities, and these cards are still not usually playable in constructed formats. Ex: Walking Corpse; Eviscerate; Strider Harness

5- A card has average efficiency, passing the vanilla test and reaching the bottom level of playability in constructed formats. These cards are more sought after but still not the best in limited formats, usually being outclassed by better uncommons and rares. Ex- Audacious Infiltrator; Lightning Strike, Angelic Gift

6- Cards have slightly above average efficiency, making them viable minor support cards in some constructed formats and decks. These cards are most of the best cards in a limited format, usually being uncommon, and as such, are picked up quickly during drafts. Ex- Doom Blade; Deadbridge Goliath, Swiftfoot Boots

7- Cards have higher than average efficiency, providing an effective and impactful effect at a relatively low cost. These are common support cards in constructed and are usually the best cards commonly seen in limited formats. Ex- Lightning Greaves, Lightning Bolt, Gigantosaurus

8- Cards with high efficiency, making them very viable support cards in any format. They are usually low-cost, high-potential creatures and removal spells, forming the core mana curve of most constructed decks. Ex- Shifting Ceratops; Phyrexian Arena, Fatal Push

9- Cards with very high efficiency. These cards usually represent the best options for providing mana advantage in each segment of the mana curve, whether they be removal, creatures, or other spells. Usually very valuable rares or mythic rares. Ex- Questing Beast; Swords to Plowshares, Mystic Remora

10- The most efficient cards in magic's history, these cards are usually considered "broken" and are banned or restricted in most formats. The bulk of these cards are from extremely old sets before card balancing was well-understood and as such they provide unmatched value in their respective categories. Ex- Black Lotus; Time Walk; Ancestral Recall

## Reliability-

How many different situations can this spell be useful in? How many decks can use at least one of its effects? Does it have relevant types? How many colored mana does it require? Can it be cast at instant speed or only sorcery speed? Is there more than one way to cast or use it? How much value is it guaranteed or likely to provide before being destroyed?

A card's reliability is determined by a number of factors. The most important and apparent factor is any secondary means of casting or using a card for a different effect than its primary function. Many cards have abilities or modes that allow its user to choose between two or more different effects when cast or activated, making the spell exponentially more effective as a whole. Many scenarios will be considered, including the worst case scenarios, in order to judge cards for this category.

Typically, a card that has more than one mode or means of use is not necessarily very adept at perfoming any of those effects in particular, meaning that, while these spells can be powerful, they will typically be less efficient and impactful than more dedicated spells. Alternate abilities such as cycling, different means of casting, and modes that provide alternate effects when cast all contribute to a card's reliability.

Another factor in reliability is mana cost. Cards that are more reliable are easier to cast in more decks. Having fewer colored mana symbols in their mana costs will contribute to a higher score in reliability- colorless spells are especially highly regarded in this category for this reason. Requiring multiple different colors in a mana cost is a detractor for reliability, though converted mana cost is a much more minor factor that is largely disregarded, unless a spell has an adaptable mana cost (X in the cost) which will boost its score.

Other minor factors in reliability include card type, subtypes, supertypes, passive abilities, active abilities, and speed of casting. Instants and flash-speed spells are considered more reliable than sorcery speed spells. Creatures are usually graded higher for reliability than other types of spells, due to their inherent multi-purpose nature, while many planeswalkers (though usually quite versatile) are graded lower because of their vulnerability without proper setup and protection. Any permanent spell with multiple activated abilities is likely to score high in this category, as are permanents with protective abilities such as hexproof, regenerate, or indestructible.

Reliability is an important factor on any magic card, and cards have historically become more multi-purpose and more reliable in recent years for the sake of mitigating luck factors in gameplay. Some cards in modern sets have been released with multiple modes, and others were even made to be used as one or both of two completely separate cards called "split" cards. These types of cards are some of the best in reliability. Cards which have narrow effects that are only applicable in very rare situations will score the lowest in this category, while planeswalkers and easy-to-remove creatures and other permanents will also take a hit.

1- A card has such a narrow effect as to only be useful in a rare situation that will almost never occur in an average game. It has no alternative options or uses and this card will be a dead card in hand in the vast majority of games it is drawn in in any format, and is nearly guaranteed to provide no value. Ex- Break Open; Root Cage; Crevasse

2- A card has only one specific use which will most likely not become useful in the average game. It is a useless card in any normal game and can only be used in an extremely niche deck, and even then is usually not justifiably useful. Most likely provides no value when played. Ex- Sanctimony; Riptide; Goblin Rock Sled

3- This card has limited uses and is only effective in a small selection of scenarios. These cards are not reliable enough to include in any constructed format, but in a specific limited format they can be useful in a pinch. Not guaranteed to provide value at any point during the game. Ex- Gainsay; Barrow Ghoul, Cold Snap

4- These cards have below average reliability, becoming useful infrequently in the average game. They can be built around to become more useful and they are useable in limited formats. Ex- Incite Hysteria; Envelop; Polar Kraken

5- These cards have average reliability, being useful in a variety of scenarios which may or may not come up in an average game. These cards are reliable enough to be staples in limited formats and are the bottom tier of usability in constructed formats. Ex- Negate; Grizzly Bears; Fire Diamond

6- A card has above average reliability. It is a useful card in a number of different scenarios, at least some of which are likely to occur during the average game. This is the highest level of versatility typically found in the average limited deck and these cards are somewhat useable in some constructed formats. Ex- Darksteel Ingot; Ghostly Sentinel; Finishing Blow

7- These cards have high levels of reliability and are useful in many different scenarios which are almost guaranteed to occur in the average game. Permanents will most likely provide value after entering the battlefield or after a single turn cycle. They are viable options to put into most constructed decks, and will rarely be dead cards for more than a few turns on curve. Ex- Sephara, Sky's Blade; Scour from Existence; Skyclave Relic

8- A card is at a very high level of reliability. It will be useful at some point in the average game, and will not spend many turns in your hand or on the field before it becomes useable. Permanents will provide guaranteed value if they are allowed to enter the battlefield. At least some cards like these should usually be included in most constructed decks. Ex- Murderous Rider/Swift End; Dimir Charm; Sculpting Steel

9- This card is extremely reliable, and is practically guaranteed to be useful within a turn cycle of being drawn. These cards are very adaptable and are highly desirable in any constructed deck that can play them, and are useful in all of them for at least one purpose. Ex- Mirrormade; Primal Command; Deathrite Shaman

10- These cards have the most reliability among any cards in magic, and are guaranteed to be useful immediately when drawn on-curve in any game. These are some of the most valuable and sought after cards, and any deck they are found in will have numerous uses for them, dramatically defining or progressing a deck's strategy or reaction policy. Ex- Mirage Mirror; Shatterskull Smashing/Shatterskull, the Hammer Pass; Walking Ballista

## Overall-

Magic is one of the most complex games in existence at the moment. There are over twenty thousand unique cards and more are created and printed for us to play with every single year. With such an enormous number, it can't be expected that every card is equal in value. Each card is unique in its own way or ways, and performs differently in every game and scenario than any other card in at least one aspect or another, but, overall, some cards are simply better than others in one or more of the rating criteria described above.

While these three criteria can help determine the strengths and weaknesses of each card, combining them all into a single overall rating can be a good indicator of how useful and effective a card is in general, compared to every other card available. While this rating is not the only thing that matters, as a card may be more suitable in a particular deck due to one of these criteria or another weighted factor, at a basic level, a card's overall score will help you sift through the masses of spells available to choose from during the deck building process.

This score will be an amalgamation of each of the three conceptual grades listed above, providing a numerical value out of a maximum attainable score to indicate the card's overall potential and performance value. A card with a score of 3 is technically deemed to be the worst rated card, while cards with a rating of 30 are the "best" cards in magic overall. The vast majority of cards will fall between these two extremes.

When deciding on which cards to include in your deck, you should decide on a certain value threshold to look at, as there are far too many cards to decide between in all of magic's history. Obviously, other criteria such as format legality and card color should be considered as well, but even with those requirements fulfilled, the amount of cards available can be extremely daunting. For limited formats, I would recommend looking at cards with at least a 10 in the overall score, and for competitive constructed formats, at least a 20. This rule is only a generalization, and can be ignored if a card is found to have a very high score in one particular area and if it also fits your deck very well, or if you simply want or need to play with under-performing cards for one reason or another.

These imaginary scores will have to be determined by you, the deck-builder, using your intuition and (hopefully) some of the principles I've listed and described above. Someday there may be a comprehensive list of all magic's cards available with scores for each of these categories, curated by an expert in card evaluation, that can be used to quickly determine the playability of any particular card, but for now, I hope that my examples will help you accurately grade and judge potential cards for your decks on your own. I encourage you to peruse the depths of the internet for further advice concerning determining the viability of magic cards and ways to improve your magic skills in general. There are many sources out there, from highly intelligent and reputable magic players that can be found on YouTube and other social/video media sites. Thank you for caring enough to learn more about the greatest game on Earth, and for reading what I have to say on this broad and fascinating topic. Happy deck-building!

## Further Examples-

This section will elaborate and expound upon the aforementioned principles of card evaluation for a few specific cards. This is for the sake of providing context for the reasoning behind how and why cards attain specific scores for each category, so as to make it easier for you to judge cards for yourself. The cards I list are randomly chosen, but most of them are at least somewhat viable in a format and are generally not too obscure or complicated, so as to provide the most helpful examples possible.

Avacyn, Angel of Hope (5)(W)(W)(W)

Legendary Creature- Angel

Flying, Vigilance, Indestructible

Other permanents you control have indestructible.

(8/8)

Impact- 8

•8 power creature: +4 points •Indestructible, Flying: +2 points •Passive board state protection: +2 points

Avacyn is a huge flyer that can both attack and block every turn. Her indestructibility makes her impossible to attack through or block effectively, making her the biggest threat on the table and a great way to protect yourself. She also gives all of your other permanents indestructible, making your entire board state relatively untouchable as long as she is on the board. She is expensive to cast (and expensive to own) for a reason- her impact is devastating and undeniable, and it is her highest score.

Efficiency- 7.5

•8/8 creature for 8 CMC: +4 points •4 extra abilities: +4 points •3 white mana symbols: -1/2 point

Avacyn is an expensive creature and difficult to cast due to her high devotion to white. Though with 4 additional abilities and a CMC equal to half her total power and toughness, she passes the vanilla test with flying colors. Those abilities being highly relevant and powerful ones, including the invaluable flying, indestructible, and a passive ability that protects your entire board. With all the text on her box, it would be very reasonable to pay even 4 mana more for what Avacyn offers; as it stands, she is a bargain, and her efficiency score reflects that.

Reliability- 7.5

•Creature: +5 points •Self-protecting ability: +1.5 points •Vigilance: +1 point •Flying: +1 point •Difficult Casting Cost: -1 point

Avacyn is a creature that is difficult to remove once she enters the battlefield due to her indestructibility. She also can both attack and block in the air, with huge power and toughness, making her both a threat and a great blocker on almost any board state. While she has no alternative means of casting or uses, and her casting cost is both high and difficult to satisfy, she is a reliable protector that can double as a heavy-hitter, and her score is high in this category.

Total- 23

Avacyn is an extremely powerful creature with high scores in every category. She is expensive and somewhat difficult to cast, but her abilities and her incredibly high stats make her an invaluable asset for almost any white deck. She is currently a staple in EDH, and her high price in USD is an indicator of her desirability. She requires no support, and there is almost no white deck in a format in which she is legal that wouldn't benefit from her inclusion.
